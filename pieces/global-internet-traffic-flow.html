Global Internet Traffic Flow

Data Visualization February 2026

Philosophy

The internet is often described as a "cloud" — an abstraction that obscures its physical reality. In truth, the internet is a sprawling network of undersea cables, satellite links, and terrestrial fiber spanning the planet. This visualization maps the invisible infrastructure: the major internet exchange points (IXPs), submarine cable routes, and traffic density between continents. It's a portrait of humanity's nervous system — the electromagnetic pulses carrying our conversations, commerce, and culture across oceans.

Visual Language

The piece uses a modified orthographic projection centered on the Atlantic, showing both hemispheres. Continents appear as dark landmasses with faint coastal outlines. The focus is on the connections — glowing arcs tracing great-circle routes between major internet hubs. Traffic volume is encoded in line thickness and luminosity. High-traffic routes (US-Europe, US-Asia, Europe-Asia) glow brighter and thicker than secondary routes.

Key visual elements: arc trajectories following great-circle paths with heights proportional to distance; hub nodes as bright convergence points at major cities (London, New York, Singapore, Tokyo, Frankfurt); faint submarine cable routes rendered beneath the traffic arcs; and traffic patterns that reveal the hierarchical structure of global internet routing.

Color

The color palette encodes network activity. Primary routes with high traffic flow in electric cyan to white at peak intensity. Secondary routes in teal at reduced opacity. Submarine cable infrastructure appears in deep blue, establishing the physical foundation beneath the data flows. Hub nodes shine as bright white cores with cyan halos, marking the convergence points where continents connect. The entire composition sits against a deep ocean blue-black background, emphasizing the oceanic cables that carry most international traffic.

Data

The visualization represents typical global routing hierarchies based on internet exchange point locations from PeeringDB and TeleGeography, submarine cable routes from SubmarineCableMap.com, and traffic volume estimates from regional internet registry statistics. The Atlantic corridor — connecting North America, Europe, and Africa — dominates the frame as the densest internet infrastructure zone on the planet. Asia-Pacific routes arc across the top, connecting Tokyo, Singapore, and Hong Kong to the global network.
