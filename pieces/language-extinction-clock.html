Language Extinction Clock

Data Visualization February 2026

Philosophy

Language Extinction Clock presents the fragility of human linguistic diversity as a temporal diagram — a clock face where each hand represents a language, and some hands are stopping. Of the roughly 7,000 languages spoken today, nearly half are endangered.

One language dies every two weeks. This piece makes that ongoing loss visible. Each line is a timeline in miniature: the full span of a language's known existence, from first documentation to the present moment — or to silence.

Visual Language

The composition is circular, evoking both a clock and a mandala. Languages radiate outward from the center as individual lines — their length proportional to centuries of documented use. A language with four millennia of recorded history extends to the outer edge.

The critical detail is at the tips. Healthy languages end in bright, solid points. Endangered languages fade: their lines thin, fragment, and dissolve into particles before reaching the rim. Extinct languages are ghosts — faint traces that stop abruptly.

Color

Color encodes language families: Indo-European in gold, Sino-Tibetan in teal, Niger-Congo in rose, Austronesian in amber, Afroasiatic in warm white, and smaller families in subtler hues. The diversity of color is the diversity of human thought.

Data

The visualization includes 50 languages from over 20 language families, with real historical data. Status indicators distinguish thriving, vulnerable, endangered, critically endangered, and recently extinct languages. A clock hand metaphor points to the present moment.
